🧠 AI Basics

What Even Is AI?

Before we dive into applications, let's build a shared foundation. Tap any section below to explore — from the quick version to the deeper dive.

AI (Artificial Intelligence) refers to computer systems trained to do things that normally require human intelligence — understanding language, recognizing images, generating text, and more.

Modern tools like ChatGPT and Gemini are Large Language Models (LLMs). They've read a massive portion of the internet and learned to predict what helpful, coherent text looks like. When you type a prompt, the AI blends what it learned from the web with what you told it — and generates a response.

Key takeaway: The more context and detail you give it, the more useful and accurate its response will be.

The most important idea of today: AI should enhance learning and teaching, not replace it.

  • Enhance learning, not replace it: A student who has AI write their essay hasn't learned anything. But a student who uses AI to brainstorm, get feedback, and revise — that's enhanced learning.
  • Enhance teaching, not replace it: AI can grade routine feedback, generate practice problems, and improve slide decks — freeing you for the high-value work only a teacher can do.
  • Don't force it: Sometimes direct instruction is simply the best approach. AI doesn't need to be in every lesson.
  • Myth: AI is always accurate. Reality: AI can "hallucinate" — confidently stating incorrect information. Always verify important facts.
  • Myth: Students will just use AI to cheat. Reality: Students who would cheat were always going to find a way. The solution is designing assignments where AI is a tool, not a shortcut.
  • Myth: AI is too complicated for me. Reality: If you can type a sentence, you can use AI. The learning curve is prompt quality, not tech skills.
  • Myth: AI will replace teachers. Reality: AI cannot build relationships, read a room, or adapt to a student's emotional state. It's a tool — a very powerful one.

Our students are already using AI — often in inefficient or academically dishonest ways — because no one has taught them how to use it well. That's our job.

When students leave BHS, they'll enter a world where AI fluency is expected in almost every career: healthcare, trades, business, arts, and beyond. Our district's mission is to prepare students to be successful in what comes next. That means AI literacy is part of our responsibility now.

We have to act fast — not because of hype, but because our students can't afford to be behind.

Illinois has some of the most rigorous student data protection laws in the country. Here are the non-negotiable "Red Lines" for AI use at BHS:

  • IEP Exclusivity: AI cannot be used as the sole basis for developing Individualized Education Programs. Human analysis is a statutory requirement.
  • PII Minimization: Collect only what's necessary. Use age ranges, not birthdates. Never input student names, IDs, or identifying details into public AI tools.
  • Immigration Status: Schools are prohibited from requesting or disclosing immigration/citizenship status via AI tools or any other means.
  • Deepfakes (effective 2026): New Illinois law requires internet safety instruction to address AI-generated false representations of individuals.
  • Vendor DPAs: Any AI tool used with students must have a signed Data Privacy Agreement. Check with administration before introducing new tools.

Safe practice: Never input student names, grades, or personal details into a public AI tool. Use anonymized or fictional examples when demonstrating AI to students.

Not all AI use is created equal. The key distinction is whether AI is augmenting student thinking or replacing it.

  • Augmentation (good): AI gives hints, summarizes complex readings, asks Socratic questions, or provides a first draft that students must analyze and revise. The student is still doing the thinking.
  • Offloading (problematic): AI writes the essay, solves the problem, or constructs the argument. The student accepts the output without engaging their own reasoning.

Research from Stanford's 2026 Review identifies a "fluency illusion" — students feel they've learned something because AI gave them a neat summary, but fail to retain it when assessed independently. Design assignments where AI is a thinking partner, not a thinking replacement.

✏ Prompt Engineering

The Skill That Changes Everything

The single biggest reason AI feels useless to most people? Weak prompts. AI blends what it learned from the web with what you give it. The more detail you provide upfront, the more it sounds like you.

❌ Weak Prompt
Write a recommendation letter for my student, John Smith.
Result: Generic, forgettable letter. Could be for any student. Says nothing meaningful. You might as well not have used AI.
✅ Strong Prompt
Write a college recommendation letter for John Smith, a junior in my AP English class. John struggled with severe test anxiety freshman year but completely transformed his approach — he now leads our writing workshop voluntarily. His analysis of Toni Morrison was the best I've read from a high school student. He's applying to UChicago for English Lit. Tone: warm, specific, confident.
Result: A letter that actually sounds like you, tells John's real story, and will stand out to admissions officers.

Don't want to type a long prompt? Don't. Here's the workflow:

  • Open Voice Memo on your iPhone (or any Android recorder)
  • Talk through everything about your assignment — the context, goals, what good work looks like, what you're worried students are missing
  • Upload that recording to ChatGPT or Gemini (or use Google Docs voice-to-text and paste the transcript)
  • The AI now has your full mental model — and responds from your perspective

This is especially powerful for grading feedback: record your rubric walkthrough, upload a scanned student paper, and ask for specific, actionable feedback. Done for every student, overnight.

AI doesn't always nail it the first try. That's normal — and fixable.

  • "That's too generic." → Add more specific context. Say: "This is for 10th graders who just read The Great Gatsby, not a general audience."
  • "That's not my tone." → Say: "Rewrite this in a warmer, more conversational tone" or "Make this sound like a teacher speaking to students, not a textbook."
  • "It missed the point." → Add a constraint: "Focus only on the thesis and evidence. Ignore formatting for now."
  • "It's too long." → Say: "Condense this to 3 bullet points" or "Give me the version a student could read in 2 minutes."
  • Nothing is working. → Start a new chat. Long conversations can confuse the model. Fresh context often fixes it.

These structures consistently produce better results:

  • Role + Task + Context + Constraints: "Act as a high school English teacher. Write feedback on this essay [paste essay] based on this rubric [paste rubric]. The student is a 10th grader who struggles with organization. Be encouraging but specific. Max 200 words."
  • The Before/After request: "Here's my current Google Slides summary slide [paste text]. Rewrite it to be more engaging for high school students without losing accuracy."
  • The Variation request: "Give me 3 different versions of this discussion question: one for below-grade readers, one for grade level, one for advanced students."
  • The Chain prompt: Start broad, then refine. "Give me 5 ideas for a project on the Civil Rights Movement." → "Expand option 3 into a full project description with rubric."
  • AI should not replace student thinking — having it write an essay from scratch is not learning.
  • AI should not be used to generate grades without teacher judgment reviewing the output.
  • AI should not be trusted for factual claims without verification — it can confidently state false information.
  • AI should not be forced into every lesson — sometimes the best instruction is still direct teaching and genuine conversation.
  • AI should not replace the teacher-student relationship — no AI can do what you do in knowing, encouraging, and challenging your students.

📚 What is TPACK? (The Original Framework)

TPACK stands for Technological Pedagogical Content Knowledge. Developed by Mishra & Koehler, it describes the three types of knowledge a teacher needs to effectively integrate technology into instruction:

📚
Content Knowledge (CK)

Deep understanding of your subject matter — what you actually teach.

🎯
Pedagogical Knowledge (PK)

How to teach effectively — methods, strategies, assessment, classroom management.

🛠
Technological Knowledge (TK)

Understanding of tools and technology — how to use them and their capabilities.

TPACK is the sweet spot where all three overlap — knowing what to teach, how to teach it, and which tool best supports that learning. It worked well for calculators, smartboards, and Google Docs. But generative AI breaks the model — it's unpredictable, probabilistic, and can produce original content. That's why TPACK had to evolve.

🧠 From TPACK to I-TPACK: The 2026 Evolution

The original TPACK framework was designed for static, predictable tools. Generative AI breaks that model — it's probabilistic, dynamic, and capable of producing original content. The Intelligent-TPACK (I-TPACK) framework adds AI Literacy and Ethical Competency as foundational pillars. Empirical evidence from 2025 shows intentional I-TPACK-based PD produces large effect sizes in teacher competence across all five domains.

⚠ Algorithmic Awareness — What It Actually Means

Algorithmic awareness is not about understanding code — it's about understanding behavior. Teachers with this awareness know three things:

📌 AI reflects its training data

Outputs reflect patterns — including historical biases. A "default" recommendation letter may skew toward certain demographics. Always review critically.

🎯 Context shapes output

The same question gets dramatically different answers based on framing. Prompt engineering is a literacy skill, not a trick.

❓ Confidence ≠ Accuracy

AI presents hallucinations with the same confident tone as accurate facts. Verification is always the teacher's responsibility.

🧠 Three Types of Cognitive Load — and Where AI Fits

Intrinsic Load

The inherent complexity of the material. Can't and shouldn't be eliminated — it's what makes learning challenging and valuable. AI should not touch this.

Extraneous Load

Mental effort on things that don't help learning — formatting, logistics, searching for resources. This is AI's primary target. Reducing it frees bandwidth for thinking.

Germane Load

The productive struggle that builds long-term understanding. This is what we protect with "Enhance, Not Replace." AI should never eliminate this.

The AI Opportunity: When AI reduces Extraneous Load (grading logistics, formatting, resource hunting), it frees students' cognitive bandwidth for Germane Load — the deep thinking that actually builds skills and long-term retention.

⚠ The Fluency Illusion — A Critical Warning

Stanford's 2026 Review identified a troubling pattern: students who receive AI-generated summaries feel like they've learned the content — but fail to retain it when assessed independently. The AI's fluency creates the illusion of understanding without the substance.

🚫 Cognitive Offloading
  • AI does the synthesis
  • Student accepts output without questioning
  • Feels productive, builds no lasting skill
  • Leads to "Digital Amnesia" without the AI
✅ Cognitive Augmentation
  • AI provides hints and perspectives
  • Student still constructs understanding
  • Productive struggle is preserved
  • Long-term retention and skill transfer

➕ Math & Science: Logic Debugging + Bayesian Knowledge Tracing

Logic Debugging

Instead of giving the answer, AI acts as a reviewer. Prompt it to identify logical inconsistencies without revealing the solution — then ask a Socratic question pointing toward the error. Preserves productive struggle while giving targeted support.

"Review this proof and identify the logical error. Do not give the answer. Ask one question that points the student in the right direction."
Bayesian Knowledge Tracing

Advanced adaptive systems use real-time performance data to dynamically adjust difficulty — keeping students in their Zone of Proximal Development. Research shows a 33.7% reduction in prediction deviation vs. traditional practice. This is what powers Khan Academy's AI tutor. For BHS: your ACT question bank can be built to approximate this by manually sorting by difficulty after categorizing by course.

🟢 The Stoplight Model

Communicating AI Expectations to Students

The biggest source of student confusion around AI isn't intent — it's ambiguity. The Stoplight Model gives every assignment a clear, visual AI permission level. Research shows districts using this system report significantly fewer "gray-area" violations and higher student trust.

🔴

Red Light — No AI

AI use is prohibited. These are tasks where the thinking itself is the learning — removing it defeats the purpose.

Examples: Tests & exams, personal reflections, original research, foundational skill-building exercises, in-class writing assessments.
🏹

Yellow Light — Limited Use

AI can support the process, but the student must drive the thinking. Disclose what AI helped with.

Examples: Brainstorming, outlining, summarizing complex readings, grammar/logic checking, generating first-draft feedback to react to.
🟢

Green Light — AI Encouraged

AI use is fully appropriate and aligned with the learning goal. These tasks mirror real-world professional workflows.

Examples: Data analysis, simulations, administrative tasks, translation for comprehension, generating multiple versions of materials for differentiation.
📋 Academic Integrity — Beyond Detection

By 2025, the educational sector has largely moved away from AI detectors. They have inconsistent accuracy and disproportionately flag non-native English speakers and students with certain writing styles — creating unfair false positives.

What actually works instead:

  • Provenance verification: Require students to cite AI tools used and their role (e.g., "I used ChatGPT to brainstorm, then wrote the draft myself").
  • Process evidence: Require Google Docs version history, showing the essay evolving over time — not appearing fully-formed.
  • Oral defenses: For high-stakes writing, have students briefly explain their argument and choices in person. If they wrote it, they can talk about it.
  • Reflection components: Add a "what was hard about this" or "what would you change" question — AI can't answer these authentically.
  • Assignment design: Tasks that require personal experience, local context, or class-specific knowledge are naturally AI-resistant.

Instead of "don't use AI or you're cheating," try reframing around the purpose of the work:

  • "The cheat is replacing your learning." If AI does the thinking, you don't build the skill — and that skill gap shows up on tests, in college, and in your career.
  • In math: Stop grading homework for points. When there's no grade attached, there's no incentive to copy. Students realize they actually need to learn the material.
  • Shift the question: From "did you use AI?" to "what did YOU contribute to this?" Students who genuinely engaged can always answer that.
  • Make AI visible: Assign tasks where AI use is required and must be documented — this normalizes transparency and removes the stigma/temptation of hiding it.

📝 ELA: Instant Essay Feedback

You are a high school English teacher reviewing student essays. Here is my assignment prompt: [paste your assignment prompt]. Here is my grading rubric: [paste rubric]. Below is a student paper: [paste student paper]. Please provide specific, constructive feedback that the student can act on immediately. Identify 2–3 strengths and 2–3 areas for improvement. Use encouraging language. Keep it under 250 words.

🎤 ELA: Voice Memo Feedback System

Step 1: Record a voice memo where you talk through: What is this assignment? What are you looking for? What are common mistakes? What does excellent work look like? Step 2: Upload that audio/transcript to ChatGPT or Gemini. Step 3: Also upload your rubric. Step 4: Paste or scan the student paper, then type: "Using my voice memo and rubric as your guide, provide detailed, personalized feedback for this student paper. Write it as if I am speaking directly to the student. Be specific, encouraging, and actionable. Max 300 words."

🌟 ELA: Differentiated Writing Scaffolds

Here is a writing assignment prompt for my 10th grade ELA class: [paste prompt]. Please create three versions of a writing scaffold for this assignment: 1) A heavily scaffolded version for students who need extra support (sentence starters, paragraph frames, vocabulary bank), 2) A standard scaffold with an outline and guiding questions, 3) A minimal scaffold for advanced students with just the essential thinking prompts. Each version should help students think through their argument — not give them the argument.

Provide specific feedback on: hypothesis clarity, data analysis quality, conclusion alignment with results, and scientific writing conventions. 📚 Science: Study Guide Builder

Using the following unit notes, create a student study guide for [unit name, e.g., "Cell Division and Mitosis"]: [paste notes or chapter summary]. The study guide should include: 1) Key vocabulary with definitions in student-friendly language, 2) A concept summary (one paragraph per major idea), 3) 10 self-quiz questions at three levels — recall, application, and analysis. Format it so students can print and use it to study independently.

🌟 Science: Socratic Seminar Designer

Design a Socratic seminar for high school students on this science controversy: [topic, e.g., "Should CRISPR gene editing be used on human embryos?"]. Include: an opening question, 6 discussion prompts that push students toward deeper analysis, 3 devil's advocate positions (with supporting evidence), a closing synthesis question, and a brief background reading summary students can reference. The goal is genuine inquiry, not debate — students should be challenged to think, not win.

📚 Social Studies: Essay Argument Scaffold

Here is my essay prompt: [paste prompt]. Here is my rubric: [paste rubric]. Create a structured essay outline scaffold for high school students that: 1) Offers 3 possible thesis statement options (not a fill-in-the-blank — actual thesis arguments students must choose and commit to), 2) Lists 3–4 types of evidence students should look for (not specific evidence — they find it), 3) Provides a counterargument prompt so students must address the opposing view. The scaffold should push student thinking, not replace it.

🌟 Fine Arts: AI Image Exploration (Gemini)

In Gemini (gemini.google.com), try this: "Generate 3 variations of a [style/medium, e.g., 'impressionist watercolor'] depicting [subject]. Variation 1: warm color palette, soft edges. Variation 2: cool tones, high contrast. Variation 3: minimal, abstract interpretation." Then use the results as a class discussion: — What did AI interpret well? What did it miss? — How does AI "decide" on composition? — What can a human artist do that AI cannot? This turns AI into a critical thinking tool about art itself.

💼 CTE: Business Plan Investor Feedback

You are a skeptical but fair venture capital investor reviewing a high school student's business plan. Here is their plan: [paste section]. Give specific, honest feedback on: 1) Is the problem they're solving real and significant? 2) Is their solution clearly differentiated? 3) Is their target market realistic and well-defined? 4) Are there obvious risks they haven't addressed? Be constructive — the goal is to help them strengthen the plan, not discourage them. End with the 2 most important things to fix before their next pitch.

🔥 CTE: Customer Objection Roleplay

You are a potential customer for this student's business: [describe the business in 2-3 sentences]. Your persona: [e.g., "a budget-conscious parent of two who is skeptical of new apps"]. The student will pitch their product to you. Respond realistically — ask tough questions, raise common objections, and push back on vague claims. Don't make it easy. After 5 exchanges, step out of character and give the student 3 specific tips to improve their pitch based on what you observed.

🌟 CTE: Market Reaction Simulation

Here is a business model canvas for a student startup: [paste canvas or describe model — product, customer segments, revenue streams, channels, value proposition]. Simulate how three different customer segments would realistically react to this business: 1) An early adopter who loves new ideas, 2) A mainstream customer who needs proof before buying, 3) A skeptic who sees a reason this won't work. For each segment: describe their initial reaction, their biggest concern, and what would need to change to win them over. Then identify the #1 flaw in the business model that all three segments agree on.

🤖 ChatGPT — chat.openai.com

The most widely used AI tool. Best for: text generation, feedback, lesson planning, rubric creation, recommendation letters, and any task that's primarily language-based.

Free tier: GPT-4o mini — very capable. Paid ($20/mo): GPT-4o — more powerful, can analyze images and PDFs.

Best practice: Start every session with context. Tell it who you are, what you're doing, and who your students are — before you make your first request.

✨ Gemini — gemini.google.com

Google's AI — fully free and integrated with Google Workspace. Best for: image generation, visual content, Google Slides enhancement, and anything in your Google ecosystem.

Key advantage: It can generate images (unlike basic ChatGPT). Great for concept maps, visual references, and creative projects.

Try it: Open a Google Doc, click the Gemini icon, and ask it to help you rewrite or expand any section of text.

📓 NotebookLM — notebooklm.google.com

Upload your own documents (textbooks, notes, articles, rubrics) and NotebookLM becomes an AI that only references YOUR sources. No hallucinations about things it doesn't know — it stays grounded in what you uploaded.

Best for: Creating study guides from your own materials, Q&A from a unit's readings, summarizing long documents, building student resource packets.

Bonus: It can generate an audio "podcast" summary of your uploaded documents — students can listen to it as a review.

🎤 Voice Memos — Your Best Prompting Tool

Typing detailed prompts takes time. Talking is faster. Here's the workflow that transforms your AI output:

  1. Open Voice Memos (iPhone) or any recorder app (Android)
  2. Talk for 2–3 minutes about your assignment, your goals, your students
  3. Upload the recording directly to ChatGPT or Gemini (they transcribe it automatically)
  4. Now make your request — the AI has full context and responds like it knows your class

Alternative: Use Google Docs voice typing (Tools → Voice Typing), talk your thoughts, then paste the transcript into any AI tool.

📄 Google Docs — Your AI Integration Hub

Voice typing: Tools → Voice Typing. Talk your thoughts, get a transcript, paste it into any AI tool as your prompt context.

Built-in Gemini: Click the Gemini icon (✨) in any Google Doc to get AI help directly inside the document — rewrite, expand, summarize, or ask questions about the content.

Great for: Having students do voice-dictation drafts that AI then helps them refine — keeps the thinking student-driven, uses AI for the editing process.
